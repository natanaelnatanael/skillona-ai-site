IRS Math Error Notices 2026 – How to Use the New Law to Fix Wrong Adjustments

The Internal Revenue Service Math and Taxpayer Help Act (H.R. 998) forces the IRS to rewrite its math error notices – including CP11, CP12 and CP13. This guide shows what must be in your notice starting in late 2026, how the new 60-day rules work and exactly how to request abatement if the IRS changed your return incorrectly.

Law: H.R. 998 – Math and Taxpayer Help Act
Effective: Notices issued from late 2026
Applies to: CP11 · CP12 · CP13 math error notices
Quick summary Read this first

Why your IRS math error notice will look different from 2026

For years, IRS “math error” notices told taxpayers that something on the return was wrong, the IRS had already changed the return and extra tax was due – but did not clearly explain what happened or how to fight it.

The 2025 Internal Revenue Service Math and Taxpayer Help Act (H.R. 998) changes that. It amends Internal Revenue Code section 6213(b) and gives taxpayers:

  • Plain-language explanations of the alleged error instead of vague boilerplate.
  • Specific detail – the type of error, the Code section, the line of the return and an itemized computation of all related changes.
  • A clearly marked 60-day deadline on the first page to request abatement of the math error adjustment.
  • Improved delivery rules so notices are sent to your last known address and, in some cases, via certified or registered mail.
  • Better abatement procedures, including written confirmation when the IRS grants your abatement request.

The goal is simple: if the IRS is going to change your return and demand more tax, you must clearly understand why, how much and by when you can fight back.

Risk radar · Who needs this guide

When a math error notice is especially dangerous

  • You received a CP11 notice – “Math Error on Return – Balance Due” – and the IRS increased the tax you owe.
  • You get a CP12 or CP13 notice that changes your refund or says there is no balance due, but you don’t understand why.
  • You never saw any math error notice, but now you are getting IRS balance-due letters or collection notices for a year you thought was closed.
  • Your notice is vague or missing a 60-day warning and you are not sure how to dispute the adjustment.

In all of these cases, the new rules under H.R. 998 give you more leverage – but only if you act within the 60-day window and keep records of every step.

Before vs. after H.R. 998

Old IRS math error notices vs. the new 2026 version

How math error notices worked before

  • IRS identifies a “mathematical or clerical error” and summarily assesses extra tax under section 6213(b) without sending a standard notice of deficiency.
  • Notices often gave only a general issue statement or list of changed lines, without clear explanation of the underlying problem.
  • Some notices omitted or buried the 60-day response window, leaving taxpayers unaware that they had a short time to dispute the change.
  • If the notice was never received (wrong address, mail issues), taxpayers sometimes only learned about the change once the IRS started sending collection letters.

What changes under the Math and Taxpayer Help Act

  • To be valid, a math error notice must now include plain-language detail about:
    • the type of error (for example, credit claimed without required documentation, incorrect computation, dependent error);
    • the relevant Code section;
    • the specific line(s) of the return affected; and
    • an itemized computation of all adjustments (income, deductions, credits, tax, penalties, etc.).
  • The notice must display the exact abatement request deadline in bold, larger font on the first page.
  • Notices must be sent to your last known address, reducing the chance that you never see them.
  • The IRS must provide a phone number for transcript requests so you can obtain more detail before responding.
  • The Treasury must test sending some notices by certified or registered mail and report how that affects response and abatement rates.
Key takeaway: after late 2026, you should be able to read a math error notice and see exactly what the IRS changed, why, how they calculated it and the last date to demand abatement.
60-day rule · Your protection

Understanding the 60-day abatement window

Under section 6213(b), the IRS can use math error procedures instead of regular deficiency procedures – but only if it gives you at least 60 days to respond.

H.R. 998 strengthens how that 60-day rule is communicated, and adds more structure to what happens when you request abatement.

How the 60-day rule works

  • The notice date starts the clock. You generally have 60 days from that date to tell the IRS you disagree and request abatement of the math error adjustment.
  • If you request abatement in time, the IRS must abate the assessment (remove it). If it still thinks you owe more tax, it must use regular deficiency procedures – which include the right to petition the U.S. Tax Court.
  • If you do nothing for 60 days, the math error assessment generally becomes final and is treated like any other tax balance due.

What H.R. 998 adds

  • Your math error notice must clearly:
    • explain your right to request abatement;
    • show the exact abatement request deadline in bold on page 1; and
    • describe the abatement process in plain language.
  • The IRS must send a separate letter when it grants abatement, explaining in simple terms what was abated and including a detailed computation of any remaining adjustments.
  • Procedures must allow abatement requests by mail, phone, in person and electronically (implementation details will depend on IRS systems).
Checklist · Do this when you get a notice

7-step response plan for IRS math error notices

  1. 1
    Confirm the notice type and tax year.
    Look at the top right of the letter for the notice number (CP11, CP12 or CP13) and the tax year (e.g., 2024 return). Make sure the notice is really about your filed return and not a scam.
  2. 2
    Find the 60-day abatement deadline on page 1.
    Under the new law, the deadline must be clearly printed, in bold, on the first page. Mark that date on your calendar – your strategy must be locked in before then.
  3. 3
    Read the plain-language error explanation and itemized computation.
    Identify what kind of “error” the IRS says you made, which line(s) are affected and how the tax, credits or refund changed. Compare this to a copy of your original filed return.
  4. 4
    Decide whether the IRS is actually right.
    If it really is a simple math or clerical mistake and you agree, you can accept the change. If you disagree or do not fully understand the adjustment, treat it as a dispute case.
  5. 5
    Request transcripts or additional detail if needed.
    Use the phone number on the notice to request transcripts or clarification before the 60-day deadline. Take notes of dates, names and any case ID numbers.
  6. 6
    Send a written abatement request before the deadline.
    Even if you also call, send a written letter (and keep a copy) stating that you disagree with the math error adjustment and are requesting abatement under section 6213(b). Use the template below.
  7. 7
    Watch for the IRS abatement letter and updated balance.
    After you request abatement, the IRS must send a letter describing what was abated and any remaining balance. Check that the numbers match and keep the letter with your tax records.
Tip: send your abatement request by a trackable method (for example, certified mail with return receipt) and keep proof of mailing that shows you met the 60-day deadline.
Templates · Copy & adapt

Math error abatement letters & call scripts

1. Written abatement request (mail or secure message)

Use this when you disagree with the IRS math error adjustment:

“Internal Revenue Service
Attn: [Address on CP11/CP12/CP13 Notice]

Re: Math Error Notice [CP11/CP12/CP13] – Tax Year [20XX]
SSN/Tax ID: [Last 4 digits]

To Whom It May Concern,

I am writing in response to the math error notice referenced above. I disagree with the proposed math error adjustment and hereby request abatement of the assessment under Internal Revenue Code section 6213(b) and the Internal Revenue Service Math and Taxpayer Help Act (H.R. 998).

Based on my records, the adjustment appears to be incorrect or incomplete for the following reasons:
[Brief explanation – for example, the dependent meets all requirements; income was reported correctly; credit was claimed with proper documentation, etc.]

Please abate the math error assessment and, if you continue to believe an adjustment is necessary, issue a notice of deficiency so that I may exercise my right to challenge the determination in the U.S. Tax Court.

I have enclosed supporting documentation for your review. Please send me a written response confirming the abatement or explaining any remaining balance due.

Sincerely,
[Name]
[Address]
[Phone number]”

2. Phone script for clarifying a math error notice

“Hello, I’m calling about a math error notice I received – Notice [CP11/CP12/CP13] for tax year [20XX].
I’d like to confirm exactly what error you believe occurred, which line or schedule is affected and how the new tax or refund amount was calculated.
Can you walk me through the adjustment step by step and note any information you need from me to correct this? Also, please confirm the final date by which I must request abatement if I disagree.”

3. If you never received the original notice

If you only discovered the math error adjustment because of later balance-due or collection letters, explain that in your letter and ask the IRS to treat your abatement request as timely based on lack of proper notice.

FAQ – Short answers about math error notices

Which IRS notices are covered by these rules?

The changes primarily affect math error notices like CP11 (Math Error on Return – Balance Due), CP12 (Math Error Resulting in Overpayment) and CP13 (Math Error on Return – No Balance Due). Other IRS letters may still use normal deficiency or collection procedures.

Does the new law cancel my tax bill automatically?

No. H.R. 998 does not erase tax balances by itself. It gives you clearer information and a stronger framework to challenge incorrect math error adjustments. You must still act within the 60-day window.

Can I ignore a math error notice if I think it’s small?

Ignoring a math error notice is risky. Once the 60 days pass, the assessment generally becomes final and the IRS can pursue normal collection actions. Even small errors can grow with penalties and interest.
