Overview

1. What actually changed in WHO‚Äôs 2026 GLP-1 advisory

The 2026 WHO GLP-1 Global Risk Advisory does not ban GLP-1 therapy. It tightens how it must be prescribed, documented, transported and marketed worldwide. Core themes:

  • global tightening of eligibility and documentation for GLP-1 therapy,
  • increased scrutiny of compounded and cross-border products,
  • mandatory justification and consent for off-label expansion,
  • full supply-chain verification ‚Äì including cold-chain integrity,
  • restrictions on weight-loss marketing claims,
  • more structured adverse event reporting and audit triggers.

Who is in scope: outpatient clinics, tele-health providers, pharmacies, compounding facilities, online wellness brands and distributors handling GLP-1 products.

!
The advisory is not optional ‚Äúbest practice‚Äù. Payers, regulators and platforms are already aligning policies to WHO language ‚Äì non-compliance becomes an audit magnet.
Risk Clusters

2. GLP-1 risk categories WHO focuses on in 2026

WHO highlights four critical risk clusters that regulators will track:

  • Counterfeit products ‚Äì online retailers, unverified pharmacies, informal import channels.
  • Contaminated / wrong-potency batches ‚Äì especially from non-accredited compounders.
  • Misuse & off-label expansion ‚Äì cosmetic weight-loss without documentation or consent.
  • Cold-chain breaks ‚Äì shipment or storage deviations that are not logged or disclosed.

Your compliance system must show that you can identify, document and act on each of these risks.

Documentation

3. Minimum GLP-1 documentation set WHO expects

To pass basic scrutiny, a GLP-1 service line now needs traceable documentation for:

  • patient eligibility & prescribing rationale,
  • off-label justification + informed consent (where applicable),
  • full supply-chain and batch traceability,
  • cold-chain temperature logs for all refrigerated stock,
  • compounding partner licences, COAs and sterility/potency data,
  • adverse event reports and escalation decisions,
  • marketing & claims review logs.

Off-Label & Consent

4. Off-label GLP-1 use ‚Äì what must be in the file

Off-label prescribing remains legal in many countries, but WHO treats it as a major regulatory risk surface. For each off-label patient, your file should contain:

  • diagnosis / indication and why GLP-1 is chosen,
  • clear note that use is outside approved label,
  • alternatives discussed (lifestyle, other pharmacologic options),
  • written informed consent, signed and dated,
  • titration / monitoring plan and follow-up schedule.

Supply Chain

5. Supply-chain & cold-chain ‚Äì what WHO expects you to prove

WHO‚Äôs advisory links many serious events to poor supply-chain control. You must be able to show:

  • verified supplier licences and accreditations,
  • COAs and independent lab testing for compounded product,
  • tamper-evident packaging and lot/batch tracking,
  • validated cold-chain storage equipment,
  • 24/7 temperature logging with deviation alerts,
  • quarantine + destruction records for suspect batches.

Cold-chain & supplier quick audit list

  • Each batch linked to a supplier verification form.
  • Temperature logs stored and backed up (minimum 5 years).
  • Deviation SOP: who is notified, how product is quarantined.
  • Compounding partners mapped to USP & local standards.
Audits & Red Flags

6. Audit triggers under the 2026 GLP-1 framework

Regulators and payers are likely to investigate GLP-1 providers when they see:

  • very rapid patient growth without matching documentation,
  • high volume of compounded GLP-1 from poorly documented sources,
  • missing cold-chain logs or inconsistent temperature records,
  • marketing promising guaranteed results or ‚Äúno side effects‚Äù,
  • off-label expansion with no recorded rationale or consent,
  • clusters of adverse events not escalated or reported.

FAQ

8. WHO GLP-1 2026 ‚Äì provider FAQ

Is the WHO GLP-1 advisory legally binding?
WHO does not legislate, but national regulators, payers and professional bodies are already aligning policies with the advisory language. In practice, non-alignment quickly becomes a liability and audit risk.
Who should own GLP-1 compliance inside a clinic?
Ideally one named GLP-1 compliance lead coordinates medical, pharmacy, logistics and marketing teams ‚Äì and owns the central documentation pack.
Can we keep offering compounded GLP-1?
Yes, where legally allowed ‚Äì but only with documented supplier verification, COAs, sterility/potency evidence and clear patient communication about risks.
How long do we need to store GLP-1 documentation?
As a conservative baseline, plan for at least five years of retention for eligibility, consent, supply-chain and adverse event records, or longer if local rules require.
