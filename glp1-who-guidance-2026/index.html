1. What WHO changed in 2026

The new WHO guidance tightens safety protocols for GLP-1 medications due to rapid global use. Key updates include:

  • mandatory baseline health evaluation before starting treatment,
  • closer monitoring of pancreatitis & gallbladder symptoms,
  • standardized tapering rules for stopping the drug safely,
  • clear restrictions on cosmetic/off-label use.

2. New safety monitoring requirements

Patients must now track and report specific symptoms more frequently. WHO lists these as required “early-danger indicators”:

  • Persistent stomach pain radiating to the back.
  • Sudden nausea or vomiting not tied to dosage changes.
  • Severe bloating or abdominal distension.
  • Upper-right abdominal pain (possible gallbladder issue).

Providers are expected to review this data at every refill or dose increase.

3. Rules for adjusting or stopping treatment

WHO recommends dose changes when any of the following occur:

  • rapid, unintended weight loss over 15% within 60–90 days,
  • recurrent severe GI symptoms,
  • blood test abnormalities (liver/pancreatic markers).

Stopping abruptly is no longer recommended; tapering is required.

4. Off-label use restrictions

WHO now advises that prescriptions must be tied to documented medical need. Cosmetic or “rapid slimming” use without supervision is discouraged.

Clinics must maintain written justification for every prescription.

5. What patients must do immediately

  • Update your doctor with a baseline symptom & weight log.
  • Document weekly side-effects using WHO’s 2026 checklist.
  • Report red-flag symptoms within 24–48 hours.
  • Review whether your use meets the new medical-need criteria.

2026 GLP-1 – Frequently Asked Questions

Does this change how Ozempic and Wegovy are prescribed?

Yes. Providers must document medical necessity and monitor patients more frequently.

Do these rules apply to non-diabetic patients?

Yes. WHO guidance covers all GLP-1 users regardless of diagnosis.

Should I stop my medication?

No. Only adjust dosing with medical supervision. Abrupt stopping is discouraged.

Will insurance coverage change?

Insurers may update criteria based on medical-need guidelines. Check with your provider.
