New York Minimum Wage 2026: $17 Downstate, $16 Upstate – What Employers Must Do

On January 1, 2026, New York’s minimum wage rises again. Downstate employers (NYC, Long Island, Westchester) must pay at least $17.00/hour; the rest of the state moves to $16.00/hour. This guide gives you the clean breakdown, plus a simple compliance checklist.

1. The new New York minimum wage numbers for 2026

From 1 January 2026, New York keeps a two-tier structure. The key change is a $0.50/hour increase in both tiers:

  • Downstate (NYC, Long Island, Westchester): new minimum wage is $17.00 per hour.
  • Upstate (all other counties): new minimum wage is $16.00 per hour.

These state rates remain well above the federal minimum wage of $7.25/hour. New York employers must follow the higher applicable rate (state or federal), which in practice means following the New York schedule.

Who is “downstate”? New York City’s five boroughs plus Nassau, Suffolk and Westchester Counties. Every other county is treated as “upstate” for wage purposes.

2. Who must be paid at least the new minimum wage?

Most employees who are covered by New York labor law and the federal Fair Labor Standards Act must receive at least the applicable hourly minimum, including:

  • Full-time, part-time and temporary workers.
  • Most restaurant, bar, retail and hospitality staff.
  • Non-exempt office, warehouse and service employees.

Some categories (for example, tipped workers and exempt salaried managers) follow separate cash wage, tip credit and salary threshold rules. Those numbers are also increasing for 2026, so restaurant and hospitality employers should double-check the new tables from NY DOL.

Common misconceptions

  • “1099 contractor” labels do not automatically avoid minimum wage rules.
  • Salaried employees may still be non-exempt and must receive minimum wage + overtime when their salary divided by hours worked falls below the floor.
  • Paying “under the table” does not change the legal obligation and adds tax and penalty risk.

3. Employer checklist for 1 January 2026

Use this short list to get ready before the new rates kick in:

  1. Map your locations. Identify which employees are downstate ($17) and which are upstate ($16).
  2. Review current pay rates. Flag every worker whose base pay is below the new hourly minimum.
  3. Update payroll systems. Change default rates in your payroll software, time-tracking tools and any manual spreadsheets.
  4. Check tip credits (if any). For tipped service and food-service workers, confirm the updated cash wage and tip credit numbers and adjust menus / service fees if needed.
  5. Adjust posted notices. Replace old minimum wage posters with the 2026 version for each location.
  6. Communicate new rates. Let staff know when increases take effect and how they appear on pay stubs.
Penalty risk: failure to pay at least the minimum wage can lead to back-pay, liquidated damages, interest and civil penalties. Underpayments add up fast when multiplied by hours, employees and years.

4. 2027 and beyond – automatic inflation indexing

Starting in 2027, New York’s minimum wage is tied to a measure of inflation (the CPI-W for the Northeast). In practice, this means:

  • Small increases most years instead of big jumps every few years.
  • Employers should build annual wage adjustments into budgets and multi-year contracts.
  • The state can temporarily pause increases in certain economic or budget circumstances, but the default path is upward.

5. Simple action plan for small employers

For a small shop, restaurant or service business, keep it simple:

  1. Confirm your county and 2026 minimum rate ($17 or $16) for every location where you have staff.
  2. Raise any sub-minimum wages to the new floor before the first 2026 payroll run that covers hours worked in January.
  3. Talk with your accountant about menu prices, service fees or other changes needed to cover the higher payroll cost.
  4. Set a reminder each autumn to check for the next year’s CPI-based adjustment from 2027 onwards.
