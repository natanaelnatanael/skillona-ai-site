1. How eviction records and tenant screening work in 2026

In most of the U.S., an “eviction record” is simply a court case filed by a landlord against a tenant (for example, “unlawful detainer”, “forcible entry and detainer” or “summary eviction”). Even if you never got physically locked out, the filing can still show up.

Three main places eviction information lives:

  • Court docket: public record in the court’s system (online or at the courthouse).
  • Tenant screening databases: private companies that scrape court data and sell reports to landlords.
  • Credit reports: usually only if there was a money judgment sent to collections or reported by the landlord.

Sealing or expunging an eviction usually means closing public access to the court case. After that, you must separately push tenant screening companies to update or delete their entries.

2. When an eviction can be sealed, expunged or hidden

The exact rules are different in every state, but sealing is most often possible in situations like:

  • The case was dismissed (landlord dropped it, you won, or you settled before judgment).
  • You won at trial or on appeal – judgment entered in your favor.
  • The judgment was satisfied – you paid and a period of time has passed.
  • Old cases – some states allow sealing after a set number of years.
  • Cases filed during special periods (for example, COVID-era protections in some places).
  • Clerical or identity errors – wrong person, wrong address, mistaken filing.

Some states automatically protect certain eviction records from being used in screening, while others require you to file a formal motion asking the judge to seal or restrict access to your case file.

3. Step 1 – Get your court file & screening reports

Before you ask anyone to seal anything, you need to know exactly what’s on record and how it appears.

  1. 3.1 Find your eviction case in the court system

    Search online with:

    • your full name (and previous names),
    • the county and city where you rented,
    • approximate filing year(s).

    Note the exact case number, court name and the final outcome (dismissed, judgment, etc.).

  2. 3.2 Get a copy of the docket and key documents

    Download or request:

    • the docket sheet (timeline of filings),
    • the final judgment or order,
    • any stipulation or settlement agreement,
    • any order that already mentions sealing or restricted access.

  3. 3.3 Order or download your tenant screening reports

    Ask the landlord or management company which screening services they use, and request copies of your reports from those companies. Also:

    • pull your credit reports to check for collections or judgments,
    • save all reports that mention eviction or “landlord-tenant” entries.

Keep everything in one folder – you’ll need it for your sealing motion and for disputes with screening companies later.

4. Step 2 – Check if you qualify under your state’s rules

Each state has its own language for eviction sealing, expungement or “limited access”. In general, you’re looking for rules that:

  • allow sealing after a dismissal or tenant victory,
  • allow sealing after a waiting period if the judgment is paid or satisfied,
  • allow sealing for older cases beyond a certain age,
  • restrict use of certain types of eviction cases in screening decisions.

Check:

  • your state court’s self-help or forms website,
  • legal aid or tenant rights websites in your state,
  • local housing clinics, law school clinics or tenant hotlines.

Your goal is to answer two questions:

  1. “Am I eligible under any of my state’s sealing or expungement options?”
  2. “Which form or motion do I need to file to ask the court to seal my case?”

5. Step 3 – File a motion or petition to seal the eviction

Once you know you’re eligible (or likely eligible), the next step is to ask the court formally.

  1. 5.1 Get the correct form or sample motion

    Some courts have pre-made forms (“Motion to Seal Eviction”, “Petition to Restrict Access”). Others require you to file your own written motion using your state’s rules.

  2. 5.2 Fill out your motion clearly and completely

    Include:

    • case number, court name, landlord name and your name,
    • date of the original case and outcome,
    • which sealing law or rule you are asking the court to use,
    • reasons why sealing is appropriate (dismissal, payment, age of case, hardship, etc.).

  3. 5.3 Attach evidence

    Attach:

    • copy of the judgment or dismissal,
    • proof of payment or satisfaction, if applicable,
    • any documents showing errors or hardship (identity mistake, domestic violence, etc.).

  4. 5.4 File with the clerk and serve the landlord

    Follow your court’s rules for filing (online, in person or by mail) and for giving the landlord a copy of your motion. There may be a small filing fee or a fee waiver option.

  5. 5.5 Attend the hearing (if the court schedules one)

    Be ready to explain:

    • what happened in the original case,
    • why sealing is fair and allowed under your state’s law,
    • how the eviction record is blocking your housing or job opportunities.

If the judge grants your motion, the court will issue an order sealing or restricting access to the case. Make sure you get a copy of that order.

6. Step 4 – Force tenant screening companies to update your record

Sealing the court record is powerful – but tenant screening companies may still show old data until you push them to update.

  1. 6.1 List every screening company that reported your eviction

    Use your copies of rental screening reports to list:

    • company names,
    • report numbers,
    • dates of reports.

  2. 6.2 Send a dispute letter to each company

    In your letter:

    • identify yourself and your report number,
    • state that the eviction case has been sealed or is inaccurate,
    • attach the court order sealing the case or showing dismissal/tenant victory,
    • ask them to delete or correct the eviction entry and send you an updated copy.

  3. 6.3 Track responses and deadlines

    Many consumer reporting laws require screening companies to investigate disputes within a set time and report results back to you.

  4. 6.4 Dispute any related credit report entries

    If a collection or judgment related to the eviction appears on your credit reports, dispute it separately with each credit bureau, attaching:

    • the sealing order (if it affects that entry), or
    • proof of satisfaction or errors.

7. Step 5 – How to apply for rentals while your record is still visible

Sealing and corrections take time. While you’re waiting, you can still improve your chances:

  • Prepare a short written explanation of what happened and what changed since then.
  • Bring proof of stable income, good payment history with current landlord, and references.
  • Offer a slightly higher deposit or a co-signer if allowed and safe for you.
  • Apply with smaller landlords who review applications personally instead of strict automatic systems.
  • Show that the case was dismissed, satisfied or is being sealed with documentation.

Landlords care about risk. If you can show that the eviction doesn’t reflect how you rent and pay now, some will look past it – especially if they see evidence of a sealing motion pending.

Eviction sealed record 2026 – Frequently Asked Questions

Can every eviction be sealed or expunged?

No. It depends on your state’s laws, the outcome of the case and how much time has passed. Dismissed cases and older, satisfied judgments are often the strongest candidates.

If my eviction is sealed, will landlords still see it?

Sealing usually blocks public court access, but private screening databases may still show old data until you dispute it with them directly and they update their reports.

Do I need a lawyer to seal an eviction?

Not always. Many tenants file their own motions using court forms or self-help guides. However, a housing attorney or legal aid office can help with complex cases or if the landlord objects.

How long does eviction sealing usually take?

It varies widely by court and state. Some courts decide motions in a few weeks; others take months, especially if a hearing is required or backlogs are large.

Can sealing an eviction improve my credit score?

Sealing the court case itself doesn’t automatically change your credit. But if a related collection or judgment is removed or corrected, your credit reports and scores may improve over time.
