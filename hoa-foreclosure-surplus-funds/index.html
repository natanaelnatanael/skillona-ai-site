Overview

1. What are HOA foreclosure surplus funds?

When a homeowner‚Äôs association (HOA) or condo association forecloses for unpaid dues, the property is usually sold at a public auction. The sale price is used to pay:

  1. The HOA‚Äôs lien (unpaid dues, interest, legal fees).
  2. Any foreclosure costs allowed by law.
  3. Other recorded liens in order of priority (varies by state).

If the winning bid is higher than the total debt, the remaining money is called surplus funds, excess proceeds, or overage.

In many cases, the former owner (or heirs if the owner has passed away) may be entitled to those funds ‚Äì even though they no longer own the property. Many people never claim this money because they assume everything was lost at the HOA sale.

2. Who is usually entitled to the surplus?

The exact order of who gets paid from surplus funds depends on state law, the type of foreclosure, and the recorded liens at the time of sale. Common patterns:

  • First: certain junior lienholders, if law gives them priority.
  • Then: the former owner of record at the time of foreclosure.
  • If deceased: the owner‚Äôs estate or legal heirs.

Important points for HOA surplus funds

  • A mortgage lender may still have rights to some or all of the surplus in certain states, especially if their lien survived the HOA foreclosure.
  • Some states require the court to approve any distribution of HOA foreclosure surplus funds.
  • If nobody claims the money within a certain time, it can be transferred to the state as unclaimed property.

3. How to find out if surplus funds exist

In some states, HOA surplus funds are clearly shown in the court or county records. In others, you have to dig deeper. Basic steps:

  1. Identify the case number or sale file for the HOA foreclosure (from letters, sale notice, or court website).
  2. Check the report of sale / certificate of disbursements filed with the court or county.
  3. Look for language like ‚Äúexcess proceeds‚Äù, ‚Äúsurplus funds‚Äù, ‚Äúoverage‚Äù or ‚Äúfunds remaining on deposit‚Äù.

If you‚Äôre not sure, call the clerk of court or trustee / attorney who handled the sale and ask directly whether there are funds being held for the case.

Quick checklist: confirming HOA surplus funds

  • Get the exact property address and former owner‚Äôs name.
  • Obtain the foreclosure case number or trustee sale number.
  • Request a copy of the final accounting or disbursement report.
  • Confirm who is currently holding the funds (court, trustee, HOA lawyer, county).

4. Typical process to claim HOA surplus funds

The process depends on whether the funds are held by the court, the HOA‚Äôs attorney / trustee, or the county.

4.1 If funds are held by the court

  1. Find the correct court where the foreclosure case was filed.
  2. Confirm that surplus funds are on deposit with the clerk.
  3. File a motion / petition for disbursement of surplus following local court rules (don‚Äôt ignore formatting requirements).
  4. Attach proof of your identity and your right to the funds (deed, judgment, probate documents, assignment, etc.).
  5. Attend any required hearing where the judge decides how to distribute the funds to claimants.

4.2 If funds are held by the HOA attorney or trustee

  1. Request written confirmation of the surplus amount.
  2. Ask for their claim form or written instructions.
  3. Provide ID and documents proving your claim (ownership, heirship, lien assignment, etc.).
  4. Keep copies of all correspondence and send important documents by certified mail or trackable email.

5. Deadlines and statutes of limitation

Every state has its own deadlines for claiming HOA surplus funds. In some places you might have:

  • Only a few months from the date of sale, or
  • Up to a few years, before funds are moved to unclaimed property.

Once funds are transferred to the state‚Äôs unclaimed property system, you can often still claim them later ‚Äì but the process will shift to the state treasurer / comptroller instead of the court or trustee.

Always ask the clerk or trustee: ‚ÄúWhat is the last day I can file a claim for these surplus funds?‚Äù and note that date in multiple places.

7. Common mistakes that can cost you money

  • Ignoring letters from the court, trustee, or HOA attorney because you ‚Äúalready lost the house‚Äù.

8. If the former owner has passed away

If you are an heir of someone whose property was taken by an HOA foreclosure, you may still have a claim to surplus funds as part of their estate. This often requires:

  • A copy of the death certificate.
  • Proof that you are the legal heir or personal representative (will, letters testamentary, small estate affidavit, etc.).
  • Compliance with local probate rules and any court requirements.

Some states allow simplified ‚Äúsmall estate‚Äù procedures if the total value of the estate (including surplus funds) is below a certain threshold, which may reduce time and cost.

9. Quick FAQs ‚Äì HOA Foreclosure Surplus Funds

Is this the same as a mortgage foreclosure?
No. This guide focuses on HOA or condo association foreclosures. In some states those sales do not wipe out the main mortgage, but they can still create surplus funds if the winning bid is high enough. A separate mortgage foreclosure may also create its own surplus funds with different rules and deadlines.
Can HOA surplus funds affect my credit?
The foreclosure itself may already appear on your credit reports. Claiming surplus funds usually does not harm your credit ‚Äì it is simply recovering money owed to you. Ask a tax professional if you have questions about whether the surplus is taxable income in your situation.
Can I claim funds if I live outside the U.S.?
Often yes, as long as you can verify your identity and right to the funds. You may need notarised documents or an apostille and might benefit from using a local attorney or recovery specialist familiar with your state‚Äôs procedures.
Do I need a lawyer to claim HOA surplus funds?
Some people handle simple claims themselves. If there are multiple claimants, complicated lien priority questions, or if you are an heir handling an estate, getting legal advice is often worth it compared to the amount at stake.
