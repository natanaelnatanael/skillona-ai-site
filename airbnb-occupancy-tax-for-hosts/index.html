Occupancy Tax for Airbnb Hosts (2025 Guide)

Basics

1. What is ‚Äúoccupancy tax‚Äù for Airbnb hosts?

In most U.S. cities and counties, guests pay local occupancy tax on short-term stays. It goes by different names:

  • Hotel / motel tax
  • Transient occupancy tax (TOT)
  • Lodging tax, room tax, bed tax
  • Tourism development or convention tax

The key idea is always the same: for stays under a certain number of days (often 30 nights or less), the guest pays an extra tax on top of the nightly rate and cleaning fee.

As a host, your main responsibilities are to:

  • Make sure the right tax is being charged to the guest.
  • Make sure someone (Airbnb or you) actually sends it to the government.
!
Occupancy taxes are separate from income tax. You can be fully compliant on income tax and still owe thousands in unpaid lodging taxes if you ignore local rules.

2. Who is supposed to pay ‚Äì you or the guest?

Legally, the guest usually owes the occupancy tax. But the law often makes the host or hotel the ‚Äúcollector‚Äù ‚Äì the one who must:

  • Add tax to the bill.
  • Hold it in trust.
  • Send it to the city, county or state on time.

With platforms like Airbnb, there are now two layers:

  • Airbnb may collect and remit automatically in some locations.
  • In other locations, you must register and file returns yourself.

Quick self-check: risk level

  • Do you know exactly which jurisdiction your Airbnb is in (city / county)?
  • Have you checked if Airbnb collects all applicable occupancy taxes there?
  • Do you have a lodging tax account / permit number from the city or county?
  • Are you filing returns (even zero) as often as required?
Airbnb vs. you

3. When Airbnb collects occupancy tax for you

Airbnb has agreements with many states, cities and counties to collect and remit some or all occupancy taxes on bookings made through the platform. When this applies:

  • Guests see a separate ‚Äúoccupancy tax‚Äù or ‚Äútourism tax‚Äù line at checkout.
  • Airbnb sends that portion directly to the government.
  • You receive payouts after tax has been removed.

However, this is rarely ‚Äúeverything is done‚Äù for you:

  • Sometimes Airbnb only covers state-level taxes, not city / county / special district taxes.
  • Some jurisdictions still require you to file a return showing $0 due (because Airbnb paid it).
  • If you take bookings outside Airbnb (direct, VRBO, Booking.com), you may be responsible for all tax on those stays.

Always verify in your Airbnb profile (and on your city‚Äôs website) which taxes Airbnb collects for your exact address. Do not assume ‚ÄúAirbnb pays everything‚Äù because you saw a tax line on a guest receipt.

4. When you must register and file occupancy tax yourself

You usually need to register for an occupancy / lodging tax account if any of these are true:

  • Your city or county does not have an agreement with Airbnb.
  • Airbnb only covers some layers (e.g. state tax) but not city / county.
  • You accept off-platform bookings (direct, VRBO, etc.).
  • Local rules explicitly say ‚Äúall short-term rentals must register‚Äù.

Registration is usually done via the city or county tax website. They issue your:

  • Lodging or hotel tax account number.
  • Filing frequency ‚Äì often monthly, quarterly or annually.
  • Online portal login for filing and payment.
Calculating & filing

5. How to calculate occupancy tax correctly

Each jurisdiction has its own rules on what is taxable. Common patterns:

  • Nightly room rate: almost always taxable.
  • Cleaning fee: often taxable if mandatory.
  • Extra guest fees: often taxable.
  • Security deposits: usually not taxable if fully refundable.

A simple formula for a city that taxes rate + cleaning fee:

Taxable base = nightly rate √ó nights + cleaning fee
Occupancy tax = taxable base √ó tax rate

Occupancy tax filing checklist

  • Total nights rented in the period (by platform).
  • Total taxable rent + cleaning fees collected.
  • Amount of tax Airbnb already collected and remitted (if any).
  • Tax due on your own bookings (direct, VRBO, etc.).
  • Copies or screenshots of Airbnb tax reports for backup.

6. Example: mixed Airbnb + direct bookings

Imagine this for a single month:

  • Your city occupancy tax rate: 10%.
  • Airbnb collects and remits city tax on its bookings.
  • You had:
    • $3,000 Airbnb bookings.
    • $1,000 direct bookings via your own website.

Airbnb will withhold 10% on its $3,000 and send $300 to the city. You report the full $4,000 gross to the city (if required) but:

  • Show $300 tax remitted by Airbnb.
  • Calculate 10% on your $1,000 direct = $100 due.
  • Pay $100 with your return.
Deadlines & penalties

7. Filing deadlines and what happens if you ignore them

Occupancy tax returns are time-sensitive. Cities and counties often have:

  • Late filing penalties (for missing the deadline even if $0 is due).
  • Late payment penalties + daily or monthly interest.
  • Ability to audit back several years and estimate tax due.

For hosts, the worst-case scenario is:

  • The city discovers your listing and sends a bill for several years of unpaid tax plus penalties and interest.
  • They may also require you to get a permit or license immediately.
!
Even if Airbnb is collecting tax, some jurisdictions still require you to file ‚Äúzero due‚Äù returns. Not filing at all can trigger automated penalty letters ‚Äì check the exact rules.
Optimisation & strategy

8. How occupancy tax affects your pricing strategy

Occupancy tax doesn‚Äôt disappear ‚Äì it‚Äôs either paid by the guest on top of your price or effectively hidden inside your rate. Smart hosts:

  • Treat occupancy tax as a pass-through cost paid by the guest.
  • Ensure total price (rate + cleaning + tax) is competitive with similar listings.
  • Avoid ‚Äúabsorbing‚Äù the tax in your nightly rate unless it helps your search ranking or conversion in that market.

Over a year, even a 10% tax mispriced or absorbed can completely wipe out your cleaning profit or part of your cash flow.

9. Using software and reports to stay on top of taxes

To keep occupancy taxes under control, many hosts:

  • Export Airbnb transaction and tax reports monthly.
  • Use spreadsheets or property management tools (PMS) that separate taxable vs. non-taxable items.
  • Set calendar reminders for each city / county filing deadline.
  • Work with a bookkeeper or CPA who understands short-term rentals.
Mistakes & FAQs

10. Common occupancy tax mistakes by Airbnb hosts

  • Believing that because Airbnb collects ‚Äúsome tax‚Äù, you never need to register anywhere.
  • Forgetting that direct bookings are entirely your responsibility.
  • Not including cleaning fees in the taxable base where required.
  • Ignoring letters from the city claiming you owe lodging tax.
  • Assuming long-term stays are always exempt without checking the exact day threshold.

11. Quick FAQs

Do I pay occupancy tax on stays over 30 days?
Many jurisdictions exempt longer stays (e.g. over 30 days), but not all. You must check the specific rule for your city or county ‚Äì the threshold might be 28, 30, or another number.
Is occupancy tax deductible as a business expense?
Because occupancy tax is usually collected from the guest and passed through to the government, it‚Äôs not a profit expense in the same way as utilities. On your books it‚Äôs typically recorded as tax collected on behalf of the jurisdiction.
What if I just started hosting and missed earlier months?
Many cities allow you to register and file back returns. It‚Äôs usually cheaper to voluntarily correct a few months now than wait for an audit and pay years of penalties later.
Does this apply if I rent just one room in my home?
In many places, yes ‚Äì if it‚Äôs a short-term stay below the night threshold, even a single room can be subject to occupancy tax. Some cities have small-host exemptions, but you must confirm locally.
