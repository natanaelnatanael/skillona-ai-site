1. How IRS refund processing works in 2026

For the 2025 tax year (filed in 2026), most refunds are still processed in automated IRS systems. Your return is scanned, matched against W-2/1099 and third-party data, and then either:

  • approved for a refund, or
  • flagged for review, correction or identity verification.

When something doesn’t match or your return hits a risk rule, the IRS doesn’t just send you a long explanation right away. Instead, the system attaches transaction codes and freeze codes to your account. These codes appear in your IRS online account and on your account transcript.

The good news: when you understand these codes, you can see exactly where your refund is stuck and what the IRS is waiting for from you (if anything).

2. Key IRS refund codes in 2026 (plain English)

The IRS Internal Revenue Manual uses many internal transaction codes, but a few are especially important for refund delays. Here are the ones most taxpayers see in 2026 and what they usually mean in plain English:

Code What it usually means What it means for your refund
570 Additional account action pending (hold on refund). Your refund is frozen until the IRS finishes a review or correction.
971 Notice issued (the IRS has sent or will send you a letter). Expect a letter explaining what the IRS needs from you or what changed on your return.
810 Refund freeze (serious hold, often for identity or fraud concerns). Refund is locked until you pass verification or the IRS clears the issue.
811 Refund freeze released. Your refund should move forward after this posts.
846 Refund issued. Your refund has been approved and a payment date is set.
570 + 971 Hold in place and a notice is on the way. Most common pattern for 2026 refund reviews – watch your mail.

These codes don’t show the full story alone, but together with dates and amounts they tell you whether the IRS is just taking extra time or actively waiting for a response from you.

3. Why your refund is frozen in 2026 – most common reasons

In 2026, the IRS is under pressure to fight fraud and incorrect refunds. That means more returns get temporarily frozen while the system checks details. The most common triggers are:

  • Income mismatch: The wages or income you reported doesn’t match W-2, 1099 or other information returns the IRS has on file.
  • Credit claims flagged: You claimed refundable credits (like the Earned Income Tax Credit or Child Tax Credit) that don’t match dependents or income patterns from prior years.
  • Identity concerns: Your return looks different from your normal filing pattern, or your information matches a known fraud pattern.
  • Math corrections or adjustments: The IRS system made changes that need a manual check before a refund can be released.
  • Past-due debts: Part or all of your refund may be held to pay child support, federal or state tax debts, or certain federal obligations.

A frozen refund doesn’t automatically mean you did something wrong. It often just means the IRS needs more time or more information before paying out.

4. Step-by-step: How to respond and unfreeze your refund

Here is a practical, 5-step playbook you can follow when you see codes like 570, 971 or 810 on your IRS account in 2026:

  1. Step 1 – Download your 2026 account transcript

    Log in to your IRS online account and download your Account Transcript for the tax year in question. Note:

    • Which codes appear (570, 971, 810, 846, etc.).
    • The dates next to each code.
    • Any amounts associated with adjustments.

  2. Step 2 – Check for IRS notices and letters

    If you see code 971, the IRS has sent or will send you a notice. Check both:

    • Your physical mail (look for IRS envelopes).
    • Your IRS online account for digital copies of letters.

    Read any notice carefully. It usually explains whether you need to verify your identity, send documents, or simply wait while the IRS corrects something.

  3. Step 3 – Complete any identity verification immediately

    If the IRS suspects identity issues, you may be asked to verify your identity online, by phone, or in person. Complete this as soon as possible and keep confirmation records.

    Delays in responding to ID verification requests are one of the biggest reasons refunds stay frozen for months.

  4. Step 4 – Respond to document requests with clear, complete evidence

    If the IRS asks for proof of income, dependents or credits, send:

    • W-2s, 1099s and other income statements.
    • Proof of residency and relationship for dependents.
    • Any documents the notice specifically lists.

    Use certified mail where appropriate and keep copies of everything.

  5. Step 5 – Track your codes for movement from 570/810 to 846

    After you respond, watch your account transcript:

    • Code 570 should eventually clear or be followed by a release code.
    • Code 810 (refund freeze) should be followed by 811 (freeze released).
    • Finally, you want to see 846 – Refund issued – with a date.

The process can be slow, but staying ahead of the IRS by monitoring your codes and responding quickly is the fastest way to get your refund moving again.

5. Timeline: How long IRS refund codes usually take in 2026

Every case is different, but here are typical time frames taxpayers report in 2026:

  • Simple 570 hold: 2–8 weeks while the IRS completes an internal review.
  • 570 with 971 notice: Depends on how quickly you respond to the notice and how complex the issue is (often 4–12 weeks from response).
  • 810 refund freeze: Can last several weeks or months if identity verification or fraud review is involved.
  • After 846 posts: Refunds are usually sent within a few business days to your bank or by check, depending on your payment method.

If your refund has been frozen for more than 12 weeks after you’ve responded to all IRS requests, it is usually worth calling and asking for a status update.

6. When and how to contact the IRS in 2026

Contacting the IRS too early (before they’ve even processed your return) usually doesn’t help. Consider calling when:

  • You’ve received a notice but the instructions are unclear.
  • You have completed ID verification and nothing has changed for several weeks.
  • Your refund has been frozen for more than 12 weeks with no new codes or letters.

When you call, have this information ready:

  • Your Social Security number or ITIN.
  • Copy of your tax return.
  • Dates and codes from your IRS transcript.
  • Any notice numbers from letters you’ve received.

Being organized and calm makes it easier for the IRS representative to see what’s happening and what can be done next.

7. How to avoid refund holds in future years

You can’t completely eliminate the chance of a hold, but you can reduce the risk:

  • Match your income: Make sure all W-2s and 1099s are included and match what employers and payers will report.
  • Update your address: Keep your address current with the IRS and the U.S. Postal Service to avoid lost notices.
  • Be consistent with dependents and credits: If something changes, keep documentation and be prepared to prove it.
  • Use direct deposit: It’s usually faster and more reliable than paper checks when refunds are finally released.
  • Protect your identity: Use strong passwords and watch for signs of identity theft that could cause fraudulent returns to be filed in your name.

IRS Refund Codes 2026 – Frequently Asked Questions

What does IRS code 570 mean in 2026?

Code 570 usually means there is a hold on your account while the IRS completes a review, correction or verification. Your refund is frozen until that review is done or a new code releases it.

What does code 810 mean for my refund?

Code 810 indicates a refund freeze, often related to identity or fraud concerns. Your refund will not be issued until the IRS clears the freeze or you complete any required identity verification.

Can I get my refund released faster by calling the IRS?

Sometimes you can speed things up if the IRS is waiting on information or if a hold has lasted far beyond normal timeframes. But calling before the IRS has even processed your return usually doesn’t help.

What happens after I verify my identity with the IRS?

Once your identity is confirmed, your account should eventually show codes indicating that the freeze has been released and, finally, code 846 when the refund is approved.
