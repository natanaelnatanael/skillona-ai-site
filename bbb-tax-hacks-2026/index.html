1. What is the One Big Beautiful Bill and when it applies

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (sometimes called the Working Families Tax Cut) was signed into law on July 4, 2025. It:

  • makes many 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) changes permanent, and
  • adds new deductions and credits, especially for working Americans and seniors.

For this guide, the important parts for individual taxpayers are:

  • “No tax on tips” deduction – an above-the-line deduction for qualified tip income.
  • “No tax on overtime” deduction – an above-the-line deduction for some overtime pay.
  • Car loan interest deduction – a deduction for interest on certain new car loans.

These rules generally start for the 2025 tax year (the return you file in 2026) and most run at least through 2026, with some temporary windows (for example, car loan interest deduction currently set for 2025–2028, subject to eligibility criteria).

The phrase “no tax on” is political branding. In reality these are deductions with caps and income phaseouts. Used correctly, they can still make a big difference to your final tax bill.

2. “No tax on tips” – how the deduction really works

The OBBB introduces a new deduction targeted at workers who earn tips. It does not mean “tips are invisible” – it means you can deduct qualified tips up to a limit, effectively cancelling the tax on that part of your income if you qualify.

Feature What the law does Key details (2025–2026)
Type of break New “no tax on tips” above-the-line deduction. Reduces taxable income even if you use the standard deduction.
Maximum amount Deduction for qualified tips up to a dollar cap per taxpayer. Public summaries describe a cap of up to $25,000 per taxpayer, with phaseouts at higher incomes.
Income phaseouts Benefit shrinks as income rises. Phaseout starts around $150,000 MAGI for single and $300,000 for joint filers, with full phaseout above those levels.
Who qualifies Workers in occupations that customarily receive tips. IRS lists dozens of occupations (servers, bartenders, salon workers, etc.). Employer reporting rules continue to apply.

In simple terms: if you earn tips and your income is below the phaseout levels, you may be able to deduct a large portion of that tip income so it is effectively not taxed at the federal level, subject to caps and other limitations.

Example – tipped worker in 2025 (filed 2026)

Suppose you are a server who earns:

  • $30,000 in base wages, and
  • $20,000 in reported tips.

If you qualify for the full “no tax on tips” deduction and your income is below phaseout thresholds, up to $20,000 of your tip income could be offset by the deduction (within the law’s cap), substantially reducing your taxable income compared with pre-OBBB rules.

3. “No tax on overtime” – limits, phaseouts and strategy

The OBBB also creates a separate deduction for qualified overtime pay. As with tips, it focuses on middle-income workers and uses caps and phaseouts.

Feature What the law does Key details (2025–2026)
Type of break “No tax on overtime” above-the-line deduction. Can be claimed even when using the standard deduction.
Maximum amount Deduction for qualified overtime wages up to a cap. Public guidance points to a cap of up to $12,500 per taxpayer, subject to later detailed IRS guidance.
Income phaseouts Same general income thresholds as the tips deduction. Phaseout typically begins around $150,000 MAGI single / $300,000 joint.
Who qualifies Employees earning overtime wages tracked separately on pay statements. Employer reporting and payroll systems may need updates for 2025 as a transition year.

For many workers who routinely take extra shifts, this deduction can effectively remove federal income tax on a sizable part of their overtime wages, within the law’s caps and limits.

Example – nurse with overtime

A nurse earns:

  • $60,000 base salary, and
  • $15,000 in overtime pay.

If eligible and under the phaseout threshold, up to $12,500 of that overtime could be offset by the “no tax on overtime” deduction, significantly reducing federal tax on those extra hours worked.

4. Car loan interest deduction – who actually qualifies

The OBBB introduces a new deduction for interest on certain personal auto loans – often marketed as “no tax on car loan interest”. It is powerful but far more limited than social media suggests.

Requirement What the law says Practical meaning
Type of vehicle Applies to certain new passenger vehicles only. Used cars generally do not qualify; the vehicle must be newly purchased by you.
Loan timing Loan originated after Dec. 31, 2024, within a defined window. Most guidance describes eligibility for loans starting in 2025–2028.
Interest cap Deduction capped per year. Many summaries cite a cap of up to $10,000 per year in interest per taxpayer, subject to income limits.
Vehicle assembly Vehicle must meet a “final assembly in the United States” test. Not every new vehicle qualifies – you may need to check VIN or manufacturer info.
Use of vehicle Generally personal use passenger vehicles, secured by the car itself. Lease payments do not qualify; interest must be on a loan with a lien on the car.

For those who qualify, the deduction allows you to subtract up to the cap in interest from your taxable income, reducing your federal tax bill. It does not reimburse your car payment; it lowers tax on the interest portion, up to the annual limit.

Example – qualifying new car in 2025

You buy a new U.S.-assembled car in 2025 for personal use, financed with a loan that generates $7,500 of interest in 2025. If you meet the income and eligibility rules, that $7,500 of interest can potentially be fully deductible under the car loan interest deduction for that year.

5. Stacking OBBB deductions: tips + overtime + car interest

The real power of the One Big Beautiful Bill comes when you combine multiple provisions. A single worker may be able to stack:

  • “no tax on tips” deduction,
  • “no tax on overtime” deduction, and
  • car loan interest deduction (if they bought a qualifying vehicle).

Illustrative scenario – tipped worker with overtime and a new car

Imagine a worker in 2025 (filing in 2026) who:

  • earns $35,000 in base wages,
  • earns $18,000 in tips,
  • earns $8,000 in overtime, and
  • pays $6,000 in interest on a qualifying new car loan.

If they qualify for:

  • up to $18,000 of “no tax on tips” deduction (within the cap),
  • up to $8,000 (within the overtime cap) of “no tax on overtime” deduction, and
  • $6,000 of car loan interest deduction (under the $10,000 cap),

then a very large portion of their extra work income and financing cost is shielded from federal income tax, on top of the standard deduction and any other credits they qualify for.

The exact savings depend on their bracket and credits, but the structure is clear: more of their “extra” income can end up in their pocket rather than in federal tax.

7. Common mistakes, traps and audit risks

New tax breaks draw attention from both taxpayers and the IRS. The most common problems are:

  • Assuming “all tips are tax-free” – ignoring caps, job lists and income phaseouts.
  • Claiming overtime that is not correctly classified – for example, mislabelled bonuses.
  • Car interest on ineligible vehicles – used cars, leases or vehicles not meeting assembly rules.
  • No documentation – not keeping pay stubs, tip logs or lender statements to support deductions.
  • Relying on social media instead of official guidance – viral posts often omit limits and conditions.

Being conservative and well-documented is usually the safest way to benefit from OBBB while staying off the audit radar.

OBBB 2026 tax hacks – Frequently Asked Questions

Are my tips completely tax-free under the One Big Beautiful Bill?

No. The law provides a deduction for qualified tips up to a cap and within income limits. Used correctly, it can effectively remove federal tax on a portion of your tip income, but it is not an unlimited exemption for all tips.

Is all my overtime pay exempt from tax?

No. The “no tax on overtime” provision creates a deduction up to a dollar limit and subject to income phaseouts. Only qualifying overtime wages up to the cap can be offset, and high-income taxpayers may see reduced or no benefit.

Can I deduct interest on any car loan I already have?

Generally no. The OBBB car loan interest deduction is limited to interest on certain new car loans originated after a specified date, for eligible vehicles, with annual caps. Used cars and leases usually do not qualify.

Do I have to itemize to use these OBBB deductions?

The OBBB “no tax on tips”, “no tax on overtime” and car loan interest rules are designed as above-the-line style deductions, so they can apply even if you claim the standard deduction rather than itemizing, subject to final IRS guidance.
