How to Claim Surplus Funds from IRS Tax Liens (2025 Guide)

If real estate or personal property was sold to satisfy an IRS tax lien, there may be money left over after the tax debt was paid. This guide explains what IRS tax lien surplus funds are, who can claim them, how to check if money is owed to you, and the practical steps to request a refund of excess proceeds.

Overview

1. What are IRS tax liens and surplus (excess) funds?

When you owe federal taxes and do not pay after notice and demand, the Internal Revenue Service can file a Notice of Federal Tax Lien (NFTL) against your property. In some situations, property is sold to help pay the tax debt ‚Äì either:

  • by the IRS (seizure and sale), or
  • by another creditor or government body while an IRS lien still exists.

If the sale proceeds are higher than the total amount owed on the tax lien and other senior liens, the extra money is called ‚Äúsurplus funds‚Äù or ‚Äúexcess proceeds‚Äù.

Example (simplified): the IRS is owed $30,000 and a property is sold for $80,000. After paying the IRS lien and any senior liens, there may be tens of thousands of dollars left over. That surplus does not belong to the IRS. It is held for the taxpayer or other legal claimants.

Key idea

Surplus funds from an IRS tax lien sale are not paid automatically. You generally must identify the sale, prove your interest, and file a written claim with the IRS or the office holding the money.

2. Who can usually claim IRS tax lien surplus funds?

Surplus funds are distributed according to federal and state priority rules. In practice, the following may have claims:

  • The taxpayer whose property was subject to the IRS lien.
  • Heirs or estate representatives if the taxpayer has died.
  • Other lienholders (e.g. mortgage lenders, judgment creditors) depending on priority and timing.

The IRS (or the officer / court holding the funds) will only release money to someone who can prove a legal interest and submit the required documentation.

Quick checklist: do you have a potential claim?

  • Property was sold and you previously received IRS lien or levy notices.
  • Sale price appears higher than the total tax and other debts on the property.
  • You were the owner, co-owner, heir, or recorded lienholder at the time of sale.
  • You have (or can get) sale details: date, amount, and case or sale number.

3. How to find out if there are surplus funds

Your approach depends on how the sale was handled. In many real cases, ‚ÄúIRS surplus funds‚Äù are tied to:

  • IRS seizure and sale (federal tax levy auction), or
  • State or county tax sale / foreclosure where an IRS lien was on record.

3.1 If the property was sold directly by the IRS

For a seizure and sale handled by the IRS itself:

  • Look at your IRS notices about the seizure and sale (letters, sale flyers).
  • Note the sale date, property description, and revenue officer / office.
  • Contact the IRS number listed on those notices and ask if there were excess proceeds from the sale.

If the sale produced more than the federal tax and sale expenses, the IRS should be able to tell you whether any surplus is being held and what steps are needed to claim it.

3.2 If the property was sold at a state or county sale

Sometimes a state or county tax sale happens while an IRS lien is still recorded. The sale is handled under state law, not by the IRS, and any surplus may be deposited with:

  • the state or county treasurer / comptroller, or
  • the clerk of court if a court oversaw the sale.

To check for surplus in this situation:

  1. Find the sale case number or tax sale ID from county records.
  2. Get a copy of the final accounting or disbursement report.
  3. Look for terms like ‚Äúexcess proceeds‚Äù, ‚Äúsurplus funds‚Äù, ‚Äúoverage‚Äù or ‚Äúfunds on deposit‚Äù.
  4. Ask which office is currently holding the surplus and how to claim it.
Tip

Even if the IRS had a lien, the surplus may now sit with a state or county agency. When people search ‚ÄúIRS tax lien surplus‚Äù, what they really need is often the state tax deed surplus claim process for that property.

4. Step-by-step: how to claim surplus funds from IRS tax liens

Once you confirm that surplus funds exist and identify who is holding them, the claim process usually follows these steps.

Step 1 ‚Äì Identify the correct office and procedure

  • For an IRS-run seizure and sale, call the number on your sale notices or contact the IRS Advisory Group for your area and ask about excess proceeds from a levy sale.
  • For state or county sales, read the surplus claim instructions on the treasurer, comptroller, or court website.

Step 2 ‚Äì Gather core documents

Typical documents you may need (exact requirements vary):

  • Government ID (passport, driver‚Äôs license, state ID).
  • Proof you were the taxpayer or owner (deed, tax bill, recorded lien, court judgment).
  • Sale information (date, auction or case number, copy of sale notice if available).
  • Any letters from the IRS or local government mentioning surplus or excess proceeds.

Step 3 ‚Äì Prepare a written surplus claim

In many cases there is a specific claim form; in others, a letter is enough. A basic surplus claim usually includes:

  • Your full legal name and contact information.
  • Taxpayer Identification Number (SSN, ITIN or EIN ‚Äì if requested).
  • Property description (address, parcel, or legal description).
  • Sale date, sale or case number, and any IRS or court reference numbers.
  • A short statement that you are requesting disbursement of surplus / excess proceeds and why you are entitled.
Sample surplus claim paragraph (adaptable)

‚ÄúI am the taxpayer and former owner of the property located at [address], which was sold on [date] in connection with IRS tax lien / levy case [number]. After reviewing sale and payoff information, it appears that there were surplus funds remaining after payment of all taxes and authorized expenses. I respectfully request disbursement of any excess proceeds for which I am legally entitled.‚Äù

Step 4 ‚Äì Submit the claim

Follow the instructions from the IRS or the holding agency. Submission channels may include:

  • Mail ‚Äì certified mail with return receipt is common.
  • Fax ‚Äì some IRS units still accept faxed claims.
  • In person ‚Äì for court-based claims, you may file directly with the clerk.

Always keep copies of your claim and proof of delivery. If your claim involves a court, there may be a hearing before distribution is approved.

Step 5 ‚Äì Respond to requests for more information

The IRS, court or agency may ask for additional documentation (e.g. proof you are an heir, a recorded lienholder, or that no other person has a superior claim). Answer quickly to avoid delays, and never send originals unless specifically instructed.

Step 6 ‚Äì Receive payment

If your claim is approved, surplus funds are usually paid by:

  • Paper check mailed to your address, or
  • Occasionally, direct deposit or wire transfer if offered.
Important

Processing time can be several weeks or months, especially if multiple claimants exist (e.g. co-owners, lenders, heirs). Do not assume your claim is denied just because it is slow ‚Äì follow up politely with the contact person or office.

5. Deadlines and unclaimed funds

Time limits vary widely. Some laws give only a short window to claim surplus funds after a sale; others allow several years before money is sent to a state unclaimed-property program.

  • If the sale was handled by a state or county, the deadline will usually be set by state statute.
  • After the deadline, surplus may be turned over to the state‚Äôs unclaimed property office. You may still be able to claim it later as unclaimed property.

The safest approach: once you learn that a sale occurred and an IRS lien was involved, start checking for surplus immediately rather than waiting.

6. Common mistakes that can lose you surplus funds

  • Ignoring notices about the sale because ‚Äúthe property is already gone‚Äù ‚Äì the surplus is about money, not ownership.
  • Assuming the IRS keeps everything automatically ‚Äì by law, extra proceeds belong to the taxpayer or other claimants, not automatically to the government.
  • Waiting too long and missing claim deadlines under state law.
  • Filing a claim with the wrong office (e.g. IRS when the surplus is now with a county treasurer).
  • Signing over rights to ‚Äúrecovery companies‚Äù without understanding how much of the surplus you are giving away.

7. About surplus recovery companies

You may receive calls or letters from people who say they can help you recover IRS surplus funds for a fee. These businesses are sometimes called:

  • surplus recovery specialists,
  • overage finders,
  • asset-location services.

Some are legitimate, others are aggressive or misleading. Carefully review any contract before signing. In many cases you can file a surplus claim yourself by following the official instructions in this guide and your local rules.

Questions to ask before signing anything
  • Exactly how much surplus do they believe is available?
  • What percentage or flat fee will they take if the claim is successful?
  • Can you cancel the agreement if you decide to handle it yourself?
  • Will they give you a copy of the final accounting and all court filings?

8. FAQ ‚Äì Short answers

Is this the same as a state tax deed surplus claim?

Not exactly. This guide focuses on situations where an IRS tax lien existed and a sale produced surplus funds. In practice, many ‚ÄúIRS surplus‚Äù cases also involve state or county tax deed surplus rules. Always check both federal notices and local tax-sale procedures.

Can I claim surplus funds if I live outside the U.S.?

Often yes. You may need a passport or government ID, notarised signatures, and possibly an apostille. A local attorney or trusted person with power of attorney can sometimes help with paperwork and court hearings.

Will claiming surplus funds hurt my credit or tax situation?

Claiming surplus funds is usually just recovering money that is legally yours. The foreclosure, levy or tax debt itself may already affect your credit and tax records; the claim itself typically does not create new negative marks. If you still owe other taxes, talk with a tax professional about how the refund might interact with existing balances.

Do I always need a lawyer?

Not always. Some surplus claims are straightforward and can be handled by the taxpayer following written instructions. But if large amounts of money are at stake, multiple claimants are involved, or you receive court papers you do not understand, it is wise to speak with a qualified attorney in your state.
