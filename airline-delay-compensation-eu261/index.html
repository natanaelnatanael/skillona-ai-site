Airline Delay Compensation (EU261) – 2025 Guide

High-level overview of your basic rights under EU rules for long delays, cancellations and denied boarding, plus simple steps and scripts to claim compensation directly from airlines.

1. When EU Flight Compensation May Apply (High-Level)

EU rules can apply when:

  • Your flight departs from an EU/EEA airport (any airline), or
  • Your flight arrives in the EU/EEA on an EU-based airline

And you experience one of the following:

  • Long delay arriving at destination
  • Flight cancellation
  • Denied boarding due to overbooking
Important

Airlines may not owe compensation if the cause was “extraordinary circumstances” (for example certain strikes, severe weather, security issues).

2. Step 1 – Collect Basic Flight Information

Before claiming, note:

  • Flight number and date
  • Departure and arrival airports
  • Scheduled vs. actual departure and arrival times
  • Booking reference and ticket number
  • Any written explanations from the airline

Keep boarding passes, emails and photos of airport screens if possible.

3. Step 2 – Check Basic Eligibility

High-level checks:

  • Was your arrival delay long enough to qualify (for example several hours)?
  • Was the cause under the airline’s control or extraordinary?
  • Was your ticket confirmed and did you check in on time?

Use official sources or simple online calculators as a first filter, then confirm with the airline.

4. Step 3 – Contact the Airline Directly

Most airlines have web forms or email contacts for EU compensation claims.

Short claim template:

Sample claim text

“Dear [Airline], I am writing to request compensation under EU passenger rights rules for flight [number] on [date] from [origin] to [destination]. The flight arrived [X] hours late due to [reason given]. My booking reference is [code]. Please confirm my entitlement and process the compensation.”

5. Step 4 – Attach Evidence

Attach or upload:

  • Copy of your boarding pass or e-ticket
  • Proof of delay or cancellation (email, screenshots, photos)
  • Receipts for meals or hotels if the airline promised reimbursement

Keep communication polite and clear. Avoid long emotional stories – focus on facts.

6. If the Airline Refuses or Ignores You

If the airline denies compensation and you still believe you are entitled, you may:

  • Reply and ask for a more detailed explanation
  • Submit a complaint to the relevant national authority or consumer body
  • Consider using a specialized claim service (they usually take a fee or percentage)
Note

Each country has its own procedure and timelines for handling complaints. Always use the official channels listed on government or EU sites.

7. FAQ – Short Answers

Does every delay qualify?

No. Very short delays, or delays caused by certain extraordinary circumstances, usually do not qualify.

Can I claim for connecting flights?

Sometimes yes, especially if they were part of the same booking and you arrived very late at the final destination. Details depend on routing and airline.

How long do I have to claim?

Time limits depend on national rules (statute of limitations). In some countries you can claim years after the flight, in others the window is shorter.
