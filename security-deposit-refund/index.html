Basics

1. What a security deposit refund actually is

A security deposit is money you pay your landlord before moving in to cover unpaid rent, damage beyond normal wear and tear and certain cleaning costs. When you move out, most states require the landlord to send you:

  • Your full deposit back, or
  • An itemized list of deductions plus any remaining balance.

Both must be sent within a deadline set by your state once you have moved out and returned the keys.

!
In many states, if the landlord misses the deadline or fails to send any itemized statement, they can lose the right to keep your deposit and may owe extra penalty damages on top of the original amount.
Eligibility

2. Who can legally claim the refund?

The law focuses on who signed the lease and who actually paid the deposit. In most states, any of the following can demand the refund:

  • Named tenant on the lease.
  • Joint tenants ‚Äì any one can claim on behalf of the group.
  • Heirs or estate representatives if the tenant has passed away.
  • Assignees with a written agreement transferring rights to the deposit.

Foreign nationals usually do not need a US Social Security Number to claim ‚Äì a passport, lease and forwarding address are typically enough.

Process

3. Step-by-step: how to get your security deposit back

The process is simple if you prepare before move-out and stay within your state‚Äôs timelines.

  1. Document the condition of the unit with photos and a video walkthrough.
  2. Clean and remove all belongings so the landlord cannot charge extra for basic cleaning.
  3. Return keys and get written confirmation (text, email or signed move-out form).
  4. Give a forwarding address in writing so the landlord knows where to send the refund or notice.
  5. Wait for the legal deadline (see examples below). If nothing arrives, send a demand letter.
  6. File in small-claims court if the letter is ignored or you receive an obviously illegal deduction.

Move-out day checklist for tenants

  • Take wide and close-up photos of every room after cleaning.
  • Photograph any existing damage that was there when you moved in.
  • Record utility meter readings (where applicable).
  • Hand over keys and get a written acknowledgment.
  • Send your forwarding address by email or certified mail.
Deadlines

4. Typical refund deadlines by state (sample)

Deadlines differ, but many fall between 14 and 30 days after move-out. Here are examples for major states:

  • California: 21 days after move-out.
  • Texas: 30 days after you move out and provide a forwarding address.
  • Florida: 15‚Äì30 days depending on whether the landlord is claiming deductions.
  • New York: 14 days after you move out.
  • Illinois: 30‚Äì45 days, depending on building size and circumstances.

Illegal deductions

6. Common illegal or abusive security deposit deductions

Landlords can usually deduct for unpaid rent, major damage and excessive cleaning. They generally cannot charge for:

  • Faded paint, minor scuffs and worn carpet from normal living.
  • Minor nail holes from hanging pictures (unless lease says otherwise).
  • Pre-existing damage that was never repaired during your tenancy.
  • Routine cleaning that should be part of turning over a unit.

Tenants abroad

7. What if you moved abroad or are a foreign tenant?

Moving out of the country does not erase your right to a refund. To keep things simple:

  • Provide an international forwarding address or trusted US mailing address.
  • Ask for payment via bank transfer, PayPal or other traceable method.
  • Keep all communication about the deposit in written form (email or letter) so you can show it to a court if needed.

Many courts allow you to file from abroad or use an agent, especially if the amount is within small-claims limits.

FAQs

8. Quick FAQs about security deposit refunds

Can I claim if I already left the country?
Yes. As long as you provide a reliable address or bank account and stay within your state‚Äôs statute of limitations, you can still demand the refund and even file in small-claims court.
What if the landlord charges for ‚Äúnormal wear and tear‚Äù?
Most states ban deductions for normal wear and tear. You can respond in writing, point to the statute and demand a full refund or a corrected itemized statement.
Is interest owed on my deposit?
Some states require landlords to place deposits in interest-bearing accounts and pay you the interest each year or at move-out. The PDF kit lists which states, rates and thresholds apply.
Do I need a lawyer to go to small-claims court?
Usually not. Small-claims procedures are designed so tenants can represent themselves with photos, the lease, receipts and copies of their demand letters.
