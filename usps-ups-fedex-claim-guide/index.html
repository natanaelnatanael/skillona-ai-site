Overview

1. Who should file the claim ‚Äì buyer or seller?

Before you start clicking around carrier websites, decide who actually files the claim. In most cases:

  • The shipper (seller) is the one with the contract with USPS/UPS/FedEx and should file the claim.
  • The buyer works with the seller (or marketplace like Amazon/eBay/Etsy) and provides photos and statements.
  • Marketplaces often have their own refund rules that can be easier to use than carrier claims ‚Äì use whichever route is fastest.

As a seller, a clean claim process makes you look professional and protects your margins. As a buyer, knowing the rules stops you being bounced between ‚Äútalk to the seller‚Äù and ‚Äútalk to the carrier‚Äù.

!
Always start with the party you paid (store, marketplace, merchant). If they refuse to help or are slow, you can then push for a direct carrier claim or a payment dispute (credit card/PayPal).
Step 1 ‚Äì Classify the problem

2. Lost, damaged, or ‚Äúdelivered but not received‚Äù?

Carriers treat each scenario differently:

  • Lost: Tracking stops updating, shows ‚Äúin transit‚Äù for too long, or carrier admits they can‚Äôt locate it.
  • Damaged: Item arrives broken, crushed, wet, opened, or with missing contents.
  • Delivered, not received: Tracking shows ‚Äúdelivered‚Äù but the package is not there.

For each category, you want to freeze evidence immediately: photos, tracking screenshots, notes of who you spoke to and when.

Immediate evidence checklist

  • Screenshot of tracking page showing current status.
  • Photos of box, label and damage (if applicable).
  • Photo of packaging materials (bubble wrap, fillers, etc.).
  • Proof of value (invoice, order confirmation, receipt).
  • Short written description of what happened and when you noticed it.
Step 2 ‚Äì USPS claims

3. USPS ‚Äì lost and damaged package claims

For USPS, only certain services are insured by default (Priority Mail, Priority Mail Express up to a limit). Extra insurance can be purchased on most services.

USPS lost package flow

  1. Check tracking and wait a few days past expected delivery.
  2. File a Missing Mail Search (online form) ‚Äì this triggers internal tracing.
  3. If it still doesn‚Äôt show up and the service was insured, file an official USPS insurance claim online.

USPS damaged package flow

  1. Take photos before discarding any packaging.
  2. Keep the box and contents ‚Äì USPS may ask you to bring them to a post office for inspection.
  3. File an online claim with photos and proof of value.

USPS tip: For higher-value items, ask the buyer to keep everything untouched until USPS says the inspection is complete.

i
USPS deadlines and documentation change occasionally. As a rule, don‚Äôt wait: start Missing Mail / claim process as soon as the package is clearly late or visibly damaged.
Step 3 ‚Äì UPS claims

4. UPS ‚Äì lost and damaged package claims

With UPS, the shipper usually must initiate the claim, especially for business accounts and marketplace labels.

UPS damaged package flow

  1. Document damage (photos of outside box, label, inside packing, item).
  2. Contact UPS or start an online claim ‚Äì provide tracking number and description.
  3. UPS may send a driver to inspect and pick up the package, or ask you to keep it for a period.
  4. Upload proof of value and wait for their investigation and decision.

UPS lost package flow

  1. Wait until the shipment is considered ‚Äúlost‚Äù by UPS (typically a few days after last scan).
  2. File a claim online as shipper, attaching value proof.
  3. UPS runs internal tracing; if not found, they adjudicate the claim.

UPS includes a limited liability amount by default (often around $100 for many services). Extra declared value raises the maximum they will pay if the claim is approved.

Step 4 ‚Äì FedEx claims

5. FedEx ‚Äì lost and damaged package claims

FedEx claims are also typically filed by the shipper, especially for commercial/online-store shipments.

FedEx damaged package flow

  1. Take detailed photos (box, label, packing, item damage).
  2. Start claim on FedEx site with tracking number and value.
  3. FedEx may inspect the package or ask you to keep it for a set time.
  4. Upload invoice or proof of value and any repair estimates if applicable.

FedEx lost package flow

  1. Confirm tracking has stopped updating and FedEx considers it potentially lost.
  2. File claim with description and value documentation.
  3. FedEx traces internally; if not found, they approve or deny.

As with UPS, FedEx has a default liability cap (often around $100) unless extra declared value was purchased.

Evidence & documentation

6. Proof you‚Äôll almost always be asked for

Regardless of carrier, claims usually require:

  • Tracking number and shipment date.
  • Proof of value: invoice, receipt, PayPal/Stripe order, marketplace order page.
  • Photos: outer box, label, inner packing, damaged item.
  • For lost items, a description and approximate value.

Value tip: Carriers pay based on actual value, not automatically the full declared amount. Overstating value can slow or hurt your claim.

Claim file you should build for every shipment

  • Order confirmation or invoice (PDF or screenshot).
  • Tracking history screenshot.
  • Photos (or note: ‚Äúno photos possible; package never arrived‚Äù).
  • Short explanation of what went wrong.
  • Claim number and all carrier correspondence.

8. Handling ‚Äúdelivered‚Äù tracking when the package is gone

Carriers often deny responsibility if tracking says ‚Äúdelivered‚Äù. To improve your odds:

  • Ask neighbours or building management and check around the property.
  • Check if there‚Äôs a photo of where the driver left it.
  • Confirm the correct address, unit number and name on the label.
  • File a carrier inquiry (sometimes called a ‚Äúdriver follow-up‚Äù or ‚Äúproof of delivery‚Äù request).

As a seller, for high-value orders, use:

  • Insurance or third-party shipping protection.
  • Clear policies explaining what happens in ‚Äúdelivered, not received‚Äù cases (shared risk vs. seller fully covering).

Escalation

9. When to escalate ‚Äì and to whom

If the carrier denies your claim and you still believe you‚Äôre right, options include:

  • Ask for written explanation of the denial and what evidence they used.
  • If you‚Äôre the buyer, push the seller / marketplace with that denial letter.
  • For card payments, consider a chargeback if the seller refuses to replace/refund and you never received the item.
  • For big losses, use a third-party shipping insurance company going forward instead of relying on carrier decisions.
Mistakes & FAQs

10. Common mistakes that kill shipping claims

  • Throwing away packaging and damaged goods before claim is resolved.
  • Filing under the wrong party (receiver files when only shipper can).
  • Lack of proof of value ‚Äì no invoice or order confirmation uploaded.
  • Waiting weeks or months before reporting, past claim deadlines.
  • Overstating value or changing your story halfway through the process.

11. Quick FAQs

Do carriers always refund shipping cost too?
Not always. Sometimes they only reimburse the item value up to liability limits. Check each service‚Äôs terms to see whether postage is refundable on approved claims.
Can I claim if I didn‚Äôt buy extra insurance?
Yes, but you are usually limited to the default liability (often around $100). Higher-value shipments strongly benefit from additional insurance or third-party protection.
Should I refund the customer before the claim is resolved?
For good customer experience, many businesses do ‚Äì especially for lower-value items. For high-value shipments, you might wait until the carrier at least accepts the claim or use a clear written policy on timing.
Can a denied claim be appealed?
Often you can submit new evidence and ask for reconsideration, but repeated appeals without new information usually go nowhere. Strong documentation up front is more effective.
