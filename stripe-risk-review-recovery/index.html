Why Stripe flags accounts

1. Core reasons Stripe triggers a risk review

Stripe is optimised to minimise fraud, chargebacks, money laundering and regulatory risk. Common triggers that send your account to the risk team:

  • Chargebacks or disputes creeping above card-network thresholds.
  • Sudden spikes in volume, ticket size or new countries.
  • Business models in restricted or ‚Äúedge‚Äù categories (high-ticket coaching, some subscriptions, etc.).
  • Mismatched or incomplete KYC data (ID, company docs, owners).
  • Large refund waves, complaints or suspicious traffic patterns.
!
Stripe‚Äôs default reaction is to protect the network, not your cash flow. Your goal is to quickly prove: ‚ÄúThis is a legitimate, well-run business with low long-term risk.‚Äù
What kind of action?

2. Types of Stripe risk actions (and how bad they are)

Most emails fall into four buckets:

  • Additional information request ‚Äì account still processing; they want more documents. Best case.
  • Payout delay / reserve ‚Äì Stripe still processes, but holds part of each payout or slows the payout schedule.
  • ‚ÄúUnder review‚Äù ‚Äì Stripe pauses decisions while risk/compliance team looks deeper at your model and history.
  • Account closure ‚Äì processing disabled; funds held for a period to cover chargebacks.

Before you reply, read the email and dashboard banner word-for-word so you know exactly which situation you are in.

First 24 hours

3. First response: what to do in the next 24 hours

Do this before sending any emotional messages:

  1. Read the full Stripe email and any dashboard notifications slowly.
  2. Write down every document they specifically ask for.
  3. Pause risky campaigns or unclear offers (aggressive ads, vague guarantees) until review is resolved.
  4. Prepare a clean 1‚Äì2 paragraph explanation of what you sell, who buys, and how you deliver + refund.

Emergency checklist when you get the email

  • Screenshot the email and dashboard status for your records.
  • Download recent payout and dispute history (CSV).
  • Collect company docs, ID, proof of address and key URLs.
  • Draft reply in a doc first ‚Äì then paste into Stripe.
Documents

4. Documents Stripe typically wants to see

The more organised this looks, the more ‚Äúreal‚Äù and low-risk your business appears.

  • Government ID for owners/directors (passport, national ID, licence).
  • Business registration docs (LLC/Inc certificate, articles, etc.).
  • Proof of address (utility bill, bank statement, lease).
  • Website URL, terms, refund policy and checkout flow screenshots.
  • Sample invoices, contracts and order confirmations.
  • Delivery evidence for recent orders (tracking, login logs, LMS access).

Explanation

5. How to write a strong Stripe-safe business explanation

Your explanation should be boring, clear and conservative. Include:

  • What you sell in 1‚Äì2 sentences, in plain language.
  • Who your customers are (B2B, consumers, geography).
  • How delivery works (digital access, physical shipping, timelines).
  • Refund and support (how long, where to contact, how you handle complaints).
  • Risk controls (3D Secure, ID checks, anti-fraud tools, manual review for big orders).

Example (digital products):

We sell digital training materials, templates and checklists to small business owners. Customers purchase through our website and receive instant access via email and a secure member area. We offer email support and a 14-day refund policy. We actively monitor for suspicious orders and refund proactively when customers report issues to avoid disputes.

Holds & reserves

6. When Stripe adds payout delays or rolling reserves

Stripe may keep your account but hold a percentage of each payout or stretch payouts to weekly/monthly if they perceive higher risk.

In that situation:

  • Ask what target dispute and refund rates they want to see.
  • Track chargebacks and refunds weekly and actively reduce them (clearer promises, better support, faster refunds).
  • Adjust your cashflow: treat Stripe payouts like delayed invoices, not instant cash.
  • Start researching a backup processor that is comfortable with your model.
Closures

7. If Stripe decides to close your account

Closure emails are usually short and firm. In practice, your options are limited, but you can still:

  • Reply once with a polite, factual request for reconsideration.
  • Ask how long funds will be held and when to expect final payout (often around 90 days, but it varies).
  • Download all statements, payout history, dispute history and export customer data from your own systems.
!
Avoid opening ‚Äúsecret‚Äù new Stripe accounts with different entities just to bypass a ban. If Stripe links them, you can end up with everything shut down and more questions from banks and other processors.
Risk reduction

8. How to reduce risk for Stripe and future processors

Even if your current account is gone, you can make the next one safer by:

  • Lowering refund/chargeback rates with clearer sales pages and realistic promises.
  • Adding explicit terms, privacy and refund policies visible before checkout.
  • Collecting proof of delivery or access for every order.
  • Using anti-fraud tools (address verification, 3D Secure, manual review of large or suspicious orders).
  • Avoiding ‚Äúhigh-risk‚Äù combinations: high-ticket + cold traffic + subscriptions + aggressive guarantees on a brand-new domain.
FAQ

9. Quick FAQs about Stripe risk review

Can I save my Stripe account once it is ‚Äúunder review‚Äù?
Sometimes. The better your documents and explanation, the higher your chances, but decisions depend on your business model and history. There is no guaranteed script that always works.
Why was I approved at first and only later flagged?
Initial approval is mostly identity and basic checks. Many reviews are triggered later by transaction data, dispute ratios and external alerts from card networks.
Can Stripe tell me exactly what rule I broke?
They may give a general reason (high risk, prohibited use, disputes), but rarely share full internal criteria. Focus on what you can change: clarity, documentation and metrics.
What happens to my funds if the account is closed?
Stripe usually keeps funds for a holding period to cover potential chargebacks. After that, remaining money is typically paid out to your bank account, unless there is suspected fraud or legal restriction.
