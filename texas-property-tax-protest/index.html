Step-by-step overview

1. How Texas property tax appraisals work

Texas does not have a state income tax, so local governments rely heavily on property taxes. Each county has an Appraisal District (CAD) that estimates the market value of your property each year, usually as of January 1.

The higher the appraised value, the more tax you pay. The good news: Texas law gives you the right to protest that value every year, and many owners successfully reduce their bill with simple, organised evidence.

Common reasons your value is too high

  • CAD used sales of renovated homes while yours is outdated.
  • They missed condition issues: foundation, roof, plumbing, flood damage.
  • Square footage, lot size or building type is wrong.
  • Your value is higher than similar nearby homes (unequal appraisal).
!
Important: you are not protesting the tax rate, only the value. Your goal is to bring the assessed value down to a realistic level using facts.

2. Critical deadlines (do not miss these)

In most Texas counties you must file your protest by the later of:

  • May 15, or
  • 30 days after the date on your appraisal notice.

Check the exact deadline on your appraisal notice or on your CAD website (e.g. Harris County Appraisal District, Travis CAD, Bexar CAD).

Deadline checklist

  • Open the appraisal notice as soon as it arrives.
  • Write the protest due date on a calendar.
  • File the protest immediately ‚Äì you can always withdraw later.
  • Keep email / fax confirmation of submission.

3. Filing your protest (simple version)

You usually have three ways to file:

  1. Online through your CAD‚Äôs website (fastest, easiest).
  2. By mail using the form on the back of the notice.
  3. In person at the appraisal district office.

When asked for the basis of protest, most owners check:

  • ‚ÄúValue is over market value‚Äù, and
  • ‚ÄúValue is unequal compared with similar properties‚Äù.

4. Collecting strong evidence

You do not need to be a real-estate expert. The ARB mainly wants to see clear, organised proof that supports a lower value.

4.1 Comparable sales (‚Äúcomps‚Äù)

Look for sales that are:

  • In your subdivision or very close by.
  • Similar size, age, style and condition.
  • Sold within the last 6‚Äì12 months.

Use public MLS sites (Zillow, Redfin) or county records. Print or export 3‚Äì8 of the best matches and highlight the sale price, square footage and date.

4.2 Condition issues & repairs

  • Photos of foundation cracks, roof damage, water intrusion.
  • Written estimates or invoices from contractors.
  • Insurance claims, engineer reports, inspection reports from when you bought the property.

4.3 Unequal appraisal grid

On the CAD website, pull up the values of 5‚Äì10 nearby properties similar to yours. If your appraised value per square foot is much higher, show this in a simple table.

Tip: keep everything in a single PDF or printed packet with a cover page and table of contents. Appraisers appreciate organised owners and are more likely to negotiate quickly.

5. Informal meeting with the appraiser

Many counties offer an informal meeting before the formal ARB hearing. This can be in person, by phone, or even via an online portal.

Basic script you can adapt:

‚ÄúI‚Äôm not asking for a special discount ‚Äì I just want my home valued fairly compared to similar properties and its true condition. Based on these sales and repair issues, I believe a market value around $X is more accurate.‚Äù

If you reach an agreement, the appraiser will give you a settlement value to sign. Your ARB hearing will then be cancelled.

6. The ARB hearing ‚Äì what actually happens

If no agreement is reached informally, you will receive a hearing notice from the Appraisal Review Board (ARB). This is an independent panel of citizens who listen to both sides and decide the final value.

What to bring

  • Your notice and hearing letter.
  • All printed evidence (sales, photos, estimates, grid).
  • 2‚Äì3 extra copies in case the panel needs them.
  • Short notes outlining the key points you want to make.

Typical hearing flow

  1. ARB chair opens the hearing and confirms your property.
  2. CAD appraiser presents their evidence and recommended value.
  3. You present your evidence and explain your requested value.
  4. Both sides can respond to questions.
  5. ARB privately deliberates and announces their decision.

Focus on data, not emotion. Statements like ‚Äútaxes are too high‚Äù do not help. Showing that similar homes sell for less, or that yours needs expensive repairs, does.

7. After the decision ‚Äì next steps & appeals

If the ARB reduces your value, the new number is sent to your tax office and your upcoming bill will be recalculated automatically.

If you strongly disagree with the ARB decision, you may have options to:

  • Request binding arbitration (for many residential cases), or
  • File suit in district court (higher cost, slower).

For large commercial properties, it can be worth hiring a specialist tax attorney. For most homes and small rentals, arbitration or simply protesting again next year is more practical.

8. When to hire a professional protest company

Texas has many firms that protest property taxes on a contingency fee ‚Äì they keep e.g. 25‚Äì50% of the first year‚Äôs tax savings they achieve for you.

Pros

  • No upfront fee; you only pay if they save you money.
  • They already know the local CAD data, comps and ARB panels.
  • They handle deadlines and hearings while you stay hands-off.

Cons

  • Part of the savings goes to them instead of directly reducing your bill.
  • You still sign paperwork and give them limited authority.

If you are an out-of-state or foreign investor holding multiple properties, using a protest company is often worth it purely for time and coordination.

9. Quick FAQs

Do I risk my value going up if I protest?
In practice this is very rare, and many CADs state they will not raise the value at ARB hearings. The usual outcome is a reduction or the value staying the same.
Can I protest every year?
Yes. Texas owners can file a protest regarding value every tax year, even if nothing major changed.
Does this help with future years?
Yes. A lower starting value now often means smaller increases later, especially if the CAD applies percentage increases to last year‚Äôs figure.
