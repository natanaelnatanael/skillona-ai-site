1. Why refunds are expected to be bigger in 2026

Analysts and the IRS expect average refunds for the 2026 filing season to rise, in some cases by hundreds of dollars per return. The main reasons:

  • One Big Beautiful Bill (OBBB). This law expands deductions and credits for working families and seniors and makes several earlier “temporary” tax breaks permanent.
  • Inflation adjustments. Standard deduction amounts, tax brackets and many credits are indexed for inflation, which usually means more income is taxed at lower rates and more credits are available.
  • Higher refundable credit amounts. Key refundable credits, such as the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) and the refundable portion of the Child Tax Credit (CTC), are higher for 2026.

Put simply, more taxpayers qualify for refundable money, and a larger share of existing credits can now be paid out as a refund even if your bottom-line tax is already zero.

Big picture: If you work for wages or tips, have children, earn a modest income or are 65+, the 2026 rules were designed to make your refund larger – but only if you file and claim everything correctly.

2. Who qualifies for a larger refund – 5 key groups

These are the main groups most likely to see bigger IRS refunds in 2026.

1) Workers with tips, overtime or multiple jobs

  • New rules from the OBBB give special treatment to tips, overtime pay and certain bonuses.
  • Combined with higher standard deductions, many workers will have less taxable income and may get more of their withholding back as a refund.
  • If your employer still withholds at old rates but your final tax is lower, the difference comes back as a higher refund.

2) Parents claiming the Child Tax Credit (CTC)

  • The base Child Tax Credit amount was increased for 2025 and is indexed going forward.
  • A portion of the CTC is refundable, which means you can get it back even if your tax drops to zero.
  • Parents with low-to-moderate earnings and qualifying children can see a substantial boost to refunds in 2026 compared with earlier years.

3) Low- and moderate-income workers eligible for EITC

  • The maximum Earned Income Tax Credit for 2026 rises again with inflation, with the top amount available to taxpayers who have three or more qualifying children.
  • The EITC is entirely refundable – if you qualify, it can create a refund even if you had little or no withholding.
  • Many eligible workers miss this credit simply because they do not file a return or assume they earn “too little” to benefit. In 2026 that is a very expensive mistake.

4) Seniors qualifying for the new “senior bonus” deduction

  • The OBBB introduced a new deduction for seniors, on top of the normal standard deduction increase for age 65+.
  • For qualifying seniors, this extra deduction can reduce taxable income by several thousand dollars.
  • Lower taxable income = lower tax = potentially larger refund if your withholding or estimated payments did not fully adjust.

5) Taxpayers claiming above-the-line deductions and education/energy credits

  • Above-the-line deductions (such as a new limited charitable deduction for non-itemizers) reduce income even if you do not itemize.
  • Education credits and energy-related credits can increase refunds for students and households that invested in qualifying home improvements.
  • Several of these credits are partially or fully refundable, which means they can directly increase your refund instead of just lowering tax to zero.

3. 2026 refund eligibility checklist (simple test)

Use this checklist as a quick screening tool to see if you are likely to qualify for a bigger refund in 2026. If you answer “yes” to any of these, you should look closely at the corresponding credit or deduction.

A. Work & income

  • Did you earn wages with tips or overtime reported on your paystubs?
  • Did you work more than one job during the year?
  • Is your household income in the low-to-moderate range (for example, roughly under the U.S. median)?

B. Family & dependents

  • Do you have one or more qualifying children under age 17 living with you for more than half the year?
  • Do you pay for childcare so you can work or look for work?
  • Did you adopt a child or pay significant adoption expenses?

C. Age & health

  • Will you be 65 or older by the end of 2025 (for the 2026 filing season)?
  • Do you pay high out-of-pocket medical expenses relative to your income?

D. Education, energy & savings

  • Did you or a dependent attend college or another qualifying program and pay tuition or fees?
  • Did you install qualifying energy-efficient improvements in your home?
  • Did you contribute to a traditional IRA or certain retirement plans?
If you checked at least one box in each category (A–D):
You are in the “high-probability” zone for a larger 2026 refund – especially if you have dependents and moderate earned income. Make sure you use software or a preparer that tests every credit and deduction you qualify for.

4. Steps to lock in your 2026 refund (before you file)

Bigger refund eligibility does not help if you never file or if you leave credits unclaimed. These steps reduce the chance you leave money on the table.

Step 1 – Check your withholding now

  • Use the IRS withholding estimator or a similar tool to compare expected tax vs. current withholding.
  • If your withholding is too low, you may get a small refund or owe at filing; too high and you are giving the government an interest-free loan.
  • Update your Form W-4 with your employer if needed.

Step 2 – Keep records for all refundable credits

  • Keep proof of earned income (W-2s, 1099s) and any self-employment records if you claim EITC.
  • Keep documents for children and dependents – school records, medical records or other evidence of residency.
  • Save receipts or invoices for education, childcare, adoption and energy improvements.

Step 3 – File even if you think you “don’t need to”

  • Many people with low income do not have a filing requirement – but they also miss refundable credits.
  • If you had any earned income in 2026 and are in a low- or moderate-income household, it is usually worth filing just to see if you qualify for EITC or the refundable portion of the CTC.

Step 4 – File electronically with direct deposit

  • Electronic filing reduces errors and speeds up processing.
  • Direct deposit is the fastest way to receive your refund; paper checks are being phased down and are slower.
  • Double-check routing and account numbers so your refund is not delayed or misdirected.

Step 5 – Watch out for refund delays and identity verification

  • Returns claiming EITC or the refundable CTC are often held briefly for verification.
  • If the IRS asks you to verify identity or income, respond quickly with the requested documents.
  • Do not respond to emails or texts claiming to be from the IRS about refunds – the IRS does not initiate contact that way.

Frequently Asked Questions (2026)

1. Why are IRS refunds projected to be bigger in 2026?

Refunds are expected to be larger in 2026 mainly because of changes from the One Big Beautiful Bill Act and normal inflation adjustments. The law expands deductions and credits for working families and seniors, increases the refundable portion of some credits and boosts amounts such as the Earned Income Tax Credit and the Child Tax Credit.

2. Who is most likely to qualify for a bigger tax refund in 2026?

Workers who receive tips or overtime pay, parents claiming the child tax credit, low- and moderate-income earners eligible for the EITC and seniors who qualify for the new senior bonus deduction are among the groups most likely to see larger refunds in 2026. People who combine several of these features, such as working parents with two or more children, are often in the strongest position.

3. Do I have to change anything to get the bigger 2026 refund?

You still need to file a timely return, claim all credits and deductions you are eligible for and make sure your withholding or estimated payments are set correctly. Checking your paystub, updating Form W-4 and filing electronically with direct deposit can help you capture your full 2026 refund.

4. Can my refund go down even if the law is more generous?

Yes. Your personal refund depends on your income, withholding, family situation and other factors. If you earn more but do not adjust your withholding, or if you lose eligibility for a credit, your refund may shrink even in a year when average refunds rise. That is why it is important to review your situation rather than relying on averages.

5. What if I normally owe at tax time – can I turn that into a refund for 2026?

Often you can. If you increase withholding during 2026 or make estimated payments and also qualify for more credits under the new rules, it is possible to move from a balance due to a modest refund. The trade-off is that more money is taken from each paycheck during the year, so you need to decide whether you prefer a larger refund or more take-home pay now.
